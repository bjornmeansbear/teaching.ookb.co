John Baskerville, born on January 28, 1706
in Worcestershire, England, was an esteemed
writing master, conservator, type designer,
publisher and printer. He is recognized as
transforming English printing and type design-
ing through his innovations in procedure and
style. Though Baskerville received a great
deal of public criticism during his lifetime, he
is now the recipient of well-deserved praise
for his masterpiece, the Holy Bible of 1761,
and for his invention of the groundbreaking
widespread typeface Baskerville.

As a rule, the books published in Early H8 were the
works of a perfectionist; wide margins, spacious layouts,
and no illustrations. Baskerville's inestigative writing led
him to focus on the process of printing as well; he created
a faster, standard printing bed and blacker, more even,
and quicker drying ink, and used woven instead of laid
papermaking (My Form). Similarly, Baskerville's typeface
embraces the idea of a clean, graceful page. His revision of
old-style typefaces, such as the popular Cading Old Style,
led to letterforms marked by an extreme contrast between
thick and thin strokes and sharper, even tapered serifs
(Merge 31). These wider, more open letterforms, in even-
bisftion with his almost minimalist page layout, created
a classic yet comfortable reading format.
